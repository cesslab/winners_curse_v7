Part I

This part has 40 rounds. In each round, you will participate in an auction. At the beginning of every auction, you will be randomly matched with three other participants from a previous experiment.

What is the prize?

In every round a prize will be auctioned off. The prize will be a lottery ticket, that means a device that generates a non-zero amount of credits with some probability. Any lottery in this experiment is characterized by two features:

  1. A value (in Credits ): a non-zero amount of credits that the lottery possibly pays off and,
  2. A probability (in %): the probability of receiving the non-zero amount of credits.

For instance, consider the following lottery ticket:

With a probability of 75% you get 20 credits,
otherwise (with the complementary probability of 30%) you get 0 credits.

In this case, 20 credits is the value and 75% is the probability. This means that this lottery pays 20 credits 75 times in 100 and pays 0 credits with a 25% chance. The outcome of this lottery is then either 20 credits or 0 credits.

Lottery Types

There will be four different types of lotteries. For each type of lottery, the computer will produce independently from each other 10 lottery tickets and you will bid for each one of them.

Lottery Ticket

Here is some information about the lottery ticket that is being auctioned. Throughout the experiment, however, the value of the lottery will only be partially known: The lottery ticket does not state the exact value v, but you learn that the value could be any integer between 10 and 30 inclusively. That is, the computer has chosen one number that could be 10, 11, 12, ... , 29, or 30 with equal chances). This number is called the Selected Value. Thus, each time you see a lottery ticket on screen, the Selected Value has already been determined by the computer, but it is not displayed. Neither you nor the three other bidders know the Selected Value. To assess how much you would be willing to pay for the lottery ticket, you should guess the Selected Value.

The Signal

To help you guess the Selected Value, we give you a signal. The signal is a number that is close to the Selected Value: Once you receive the signal, you can assume that the Selected Value is no more than x numbers away.

Signal Interpretation

If x = 4, a signal of 22 would mean that the Selected Value lies between 18 and 26 inclusively, where every integer between 18 and 26 is equally likely to be the Selected Value. To help you interpret the signal better, we will always display next to your signal the range of possible Selected Values given your signal.

General Remark

In every auction, you and each of the other three bidders will independently from each other receive a signal surrounding the same Selected Value. The four of you might therefore receive different signals. Yet, no one will see the signals of the other three bidders. Thus, the four of you will face the same lottery ticket, but the signal-updated range for the Selected Value may be different.

Your Task / Your Bid

In each auction, we ask you to submit a bid. A bid is the price that you are willing to pay for the lottery ticket. The three other participants have also submitted a bid for the exact same lottery ticket.
